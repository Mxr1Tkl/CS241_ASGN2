The Indenture System

One of the problems facing Sir Arthur Gordon in 1875 was how Fiji could develop its economy in order to help it pay its way. The Imperial Government had given the colony a grant of ¬£100000, but when that was spent Fiji was expected to find its own finances.

The answer obviously lay in the development of crops, especially cotton, copra and sugarcane, However, these were plantation crops hich required large amounts of labour. By this time, the import of Pacific labourers was lessening as a result of the controls placed on it, and Gordon was firmly opposed to using Fijian labour. He argued that to do so would break up the village life of the Fijian people and undermine their whole communal way of living.

Instead, Gordon recruited Indian labour, and thus changed the course of Fiji's future. Gordon's solution was not a new one, for Indian labour had been recruited for this type of work Natal in 1838 and subsequently in Trinidad, British Guiana, Mauritius and Dutch Guinea. Gordon's service in Trinidad and Mauritius, where the indenture system had worked satisfactorily, led him to believe hat Fiji would be no different. Gordon's desire not to disrupt the Fijian way S life was challenged by an opposite view which held that he Fijians would progress only when they were introduced to the modern world and changed to a way of life based on cash rather than communal living. Gordon's view prevailed, and the indenture system began.

Terms

Arrangements were made in 1878 with the Indian Government for Indian labourers to be brought to Fiji for e period of five years. The labourers were to work as directed by the government, and were to be free to return to India at their own expense at the end of five years. If they elected to stay in Fiji for another five years, their return passages, as well as those of their children, would be paid by the Fiji Government. If they wished at the end of ten years to stay in Fiji, they could do so.

The Indian Government insisted that for every hundred men sent to Fiji, forty women should also be sent. So, on 14 May 1879, the Leonidas landed the first shipload of 498 labourers from India.

Recruitment

Indians came to Fiji for a great variety of reasons. Poverty and the chance to make a fresh start were the greatest incentives, although some came to escape family quarrels or trouble with the authorities. Very few Indians came to Fiji simply because of a desire to emigrate. In fact, a journey across the seas meant a loss of caste membership for many Indians, and any journey involved expensive feasts and ceremonies of purification when the traveller returned to India.

Labourers were engaged by subagents in India who received a commission for every labourer who reached Fiji. The main work was done by recruiters whose job was to explain the terms of the contract and to see that the emigrant was properly enrolled before a magistrate and had a thorough medical examination.

It was difficult to break down the reluctance of Indians to travel, so many recruiters resorted to tricks in order to entice people to sign on: they exaggerated the fine prospects a labourer vould have in Fiji; they failed to explain all parts of the contract, especially that part which laid down a penalty if the labourer should not complete his contract; and they told those who feared the long sea journey that Fiji was very close to India, even that it was part of India itself! Altogether, over 60, 500 labourers were signed up.

The System in Practice

The labourers were housed in 'lines', rows of barracks containing sixteen rooms, each three metres by three and a half metres. These rooms were in double rows, eight in each row, with partitions which stopped short of the ceiling. Each room housed either three single men, or a married man and his wife with up to three children. All living, cooking, eating, and sleeping was carried out in this one room in which the inhabitants could not escape from the noise f their neighbours and where they could gain very little privacy. Because conditions were so overcrowded, life was very tough, and crime and immorality were soon widespread.

The quota of forty women for every hundred men was not kept up. Rumours, accusations, jealousy and fighting were common. No provision was made for educating the children of the labourers, and checks on conditions by the government inspector were both infrequent and inadequate. There was also e lack of medical care, which resulted in a high death rate. The pressure of life on the lines was also illustrated by the large number of suicides which occurred.

Work done in the fields varied according to the time of the year. The labourers cut cane during harvesting, and planted, weeded and cleared drains at other times. Work was often done on the 'task system' by which the workers' allocations for the day were measured out, and they were required to complete certain tasks before nightfall. I they failed, they would receive no wages for that day and were liable to be prosecuted. AS overseers and sardars (foreman) were usually paid a bonus if the work was done quickly and cheaply, they tended to over-task the workers, setting tasks which could be completed only by the strongest. However, closer checks on the system after 1905 greatly reduced the amount of over-tasking.

Corporal punishment was often used in the field by overseers and sardars to extract the maximum amount of work, and most workers were afraid to report such abuse for fear of reprisals. Even when the government nspectors came, they seldom received true picture of working conditions because of such fear. When a complaint was lodged and it reached the courts, the workers were still at a disadvantage. They understood little of the workings of the courts and were unable to match the legal aid available to the overseer or sardar. Most cases in which a worker's plea was upheld saw a sentence imposed on the overseer or sardar which was often so light that true justice was seldom seen to be done.

Because of the methods they used, sardars were frequently assaulted, Many laboufers also were prosecuted for failure to complete their tasks,and they were convicted in nearly every case.
