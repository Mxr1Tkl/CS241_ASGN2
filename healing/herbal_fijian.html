Medicinal Plants

Fijian Medicinal Plants and Their Role in Prevention

Abstract

Medicinal plants have historically been a critical source of therapeutic compounds and continue to play an important role in global healthcare systems. In Fiji, many communities depend on herbal medicine for primary healthcare. This review highlights the diversity of species used, their cultural relevance, and evidence for biological activity. The article stresses that while traditional practices are strong, more scientific validation is needed to establish safety and efficacy.

Background

Traditional medicine in Fiji is deeply tied to cultural knowledge passed down through generations. Rural and maritime communities in particular rely on plant-based remedies because modern health services may be limited by geography. These practices address conditions such as respiratory infections, digestive disorders, skin conditions, fever, wounds, and reproductive health. Plants such as Piper methysticum (kava) and Morinda citrifolia (noni) are prominent in both cultural and medicinal contexts. Despite increased use of pharmaceuticals, herbal medicine remains central to community identity and resilience.

Methods

The authors conducted a comprehensive review of published ethnobotanical surveys, pharmacological studies, and phytochemical analyses. They cross-referenced plant species recorded in Fiji with reported medicinal uses and laboratory evidence of biological activity. The focus was on identifying species used in multiple villages, their frequency of mention, and evidence supporting their claimed benefits. Information on preparation methods ‚Äî such as decoctions, infusions, poultices, and topical applications ‚Äî was also compared across sources.

The findings emphasize that Fiji‚Äôs medicinal flora represents a valuable healthcare resource and a repository of bioactive compounds with potential for drug development.
