Gau Islands' Medicinal Plants

Traditional knowledge of medicinal plants on Gau Island, Fiji

This article summarizes a field study from Gau Island, Fiji, that documents how local people use medicinal plants and how social and environmental factors shape those traditional resource management (TRM) practices. The authors surveyed 16 villages, recorded plant uses and remedies, and analysed relationships between community traits and sustainable resource use.

Background

On Gau Island, traditional herbal remedies have long supported everyday health needs, particularly where formal medical services are scarce. While modern healthcare has become more available, younger generations show increasing preference for pharmaceutical medicines, which threatens the transmission of local medicinal knowledge. Simultaneously, land-use changes such as forest clearing for cash crops can reduce the availability of plants used in traditional remedies.

Study aim and approach

The researchers aimed to catalog medicinal plants used across Gau‚Äôs 16 villages and to identify social and ecological factors associated with sustainable TRM. From September 2013 to January 2015 they conducted semi-structured interviews with knowledgeable community members (usually elders and medicine providers), recording the plants, parts used, prescriptions, treated symptoms, habitat, and perceived abundance. They then applied statistical analysis to test links between TRM status and community-level factors.

Key findings

The survey documented 58 commonly used medicinal species treating about 27 health issues. Two plants ‚Äî Ageratum conyzoides (locally "Botebotekoro") and Centella asiatica ("Totodro") ‚Äî were reported in all communities for various ailments. Although many species were shared, individual villages often had distinct recipes and practices for the same ailments, showing strong local variation in medicinal knowledge.

Analyses indicated that stronger community cohesion (solidarity) correlated with more sustainable TRM practices. Geographic separation, local customs, availability of land and plants, and differing economic choices (for example clearing forest for agriculture) also helped explain village-to-village differences in plant use and conservation.

Conclusions and implications

The authors argue that documenting plant uses alone is insufficient to secure future transmission of traditional knowledge. Cultural activities and social structures that reinforce community ties help maintain sustainable resource management. Conservation efforts that ignore the social context risk losing both plant biodiversity and the ethnomedical knowledge embedded in community life.

In short protecting medicinal plant diversity on Gau requires attention to social practices and local governance as well as to biological inventories.
